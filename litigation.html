Litigation Context

How opioid lawsuits shaped the path to Chapter 11

This page focuses specifically on the opioid-related allegations, settlements and trust structures that sit behind the Mallinckrodt and Endo bankruptcies and the later Keenova/Par Health structure.

Mallinckrodt: generic opioids and Acthar-related scrutiny

State AG settlements and bankruptcy-driven trust funding

  • Largest generic opioid manufacturer (per AGs)
  • Global opioid settlement framework
  • MDT II “hub” trust

Several state attorneys general describe Mallinckrodt as a major manufacturer of generic opioid pills that were widely distributed in the U.S. A 2020 global settlement framework announced by state AGs such as California and North Carolina states that Mallinckrodt agreed to pay about $1.6 billion in structured cash payments into a trust to fund opioid abatement efforts by states, local governments and tribes. (CA & NC AGs)

The California Attorney General later summarizes the bankruptcy plan as requiring Mallinckrodt to pay approximately $1.725 billion over eight years into a trust system for public and private opioid creditors, starting with a $450 million payment in June 2022. (CA AG) The Mallinckrodt Opioid Master Disbursement Trust II (MDT II) website describes MDT II as a “hub” trust that receives settlement funds and allocates them to a series of sub-trusts for different creditor groups (states, tribes, hospitals, individuals). (MDT II)

Reporting from the Washington Post and Reuters notes that when Mallinckrodt returned to Chapter 11 in 2023, the company and MDT II negotiated a revised deal in which Mallinckrodt would make a one-time $250 million payment and eliminate nearly $1 billion of remaining opioid settlement obligations, while also reducing other debt by roughly $2 billion. (Washington Post, Reuters, MDT II)

Separately, the U.S. Department of Justice and several state AGs pursued Mallinckrodt over Medicaid rebates tied to Acthar Gel, a non-opioid product. DOJ and state releases explain that Mallinckrodt and its predecessor Questcor allegedly underpaid rebates by treating Acthar as if it were a “new drug” in 2013 rather than a product originally approved decades earlier, resulting in large additional payments and civil penalties. (DOJ, WA & NJ AGs)

Endo: Opana ER, marketing conduct and federal resolution

State claims plus a major federal criminal case

  • Opana & Opana ER
  • Marketing & misbranding allegations
  • Endo Public Opioid Trust

State complaints such as the one filed by the Kentucky Attorney General allege that Endo developed, marketed and sold opioids including Opana and Opana ER while downplaying addiction risks and overstating benefits, contributing to the opioid epidemic in those jurisdictions. (Kentucky AG) In 2017, the FDA asked Endo to withdraw Opana ER from the market, and Endo complied. (FDA references in AG filings)

In 2024, the U.S. Department of Justice announced that Endo Health Solutions was ordered to pay more than $1.5 billion in criminal fines and forfeiture for distributing misbranded Opana ER, describing this as one of the largest criminal financial penalties ever imposed on a pharmaceutical company. (DOJ) In parallel, a bankruptcy settlement provided that Endo would pay roughly $465 million to resolve criminal and civil claims with the U.S. government and about $450 million to state and municipal opioid trusts, as summarized by the Financial Times and other coverage. (FT, Reuters, Law360 summaries)

Endo’s confirmed Chapter 11 plan, as described by Reuters and by state AG materials, dedicates around $600 million to opioid and tax-related settlements and creates the Endo Public Opioid Trust for state-level abatement payments, while transferring more than 95% of the reorganized equity to Endo’s secured lenders. (Reuters, CA AG, Endo Public Opioid Trust)

The Endo Public Opioid Trust site explains that the trust’s beneficiaries are U.S. states, the District of Columbia and certain territories, and that the trustee is responsible for administering distributions in line with the Endo plan and trust agreement. (Endo Public Opioid Trust)

Combined Story

How the litigation connects to the merger

Financial Times and other outlets explicitly link the merger to the two companies’ opioid-era liabilities and their use of Chapter 11 to resolve them.

A March 2025 article in the Financial Times describes Mallinckrodt and Endo as drugmakers “emerging from bankruptcy processes linked to liabilities over the US opioid crisis,” noting that both companies had resolved billions of dollars in claims related to their distribution of addictive generic opioid painkillers before agreeing to combine in a nearly $7 billion transaction. (FT)

According to the FT report, Mallinckrodt paid about $700 million in 2023 to settle lawsuits related to its generic oxycodone products, while Endo agreed to pay $465 million to the U.S. government and $450 million to state and municipal trusts, and pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor over its opioid marketing practices, with additional obligations flowing through its bankruptcy plan and the Endo Public Opioid Trust. (FT)

The article groups Mallinckrodt and Endo with Purdue Pharma as examples of drugmakers that used Chapter 11 to settle opioid claims and continue operating. The restructuring architecture puts opioid claimants into trust vehicles while leaving restructured operating businesses — now branded Keenova and Par Health — to focus on ongoing pharmaceutical manufacturing and development.

Operational vs. legacy liability.
Post-restructuring, Keenova and Par Health are presented in public materials as going-concern operating companies, while large parts of the opioid liability streams are channeled into MDT II and the Endo Public Opioid Trust, funded over time under the bankruptcy plans.
Persistent reputational risk.
Even with the legal structures in place, financial press coverage highlights ongoing scrutiny of how Chapter 11 has been used by opioid manufacturers and how stakeholders perceive the balance between victim compensation and corporate survival.
