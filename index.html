Combined transaction snapshot

In March 2025, Mallinckrodt and Endo agreed to merge in a deal valued at nearly $7 billion, combining two specialty drugmakers that had each emerged from Chapter 11 processes linked to opioid-related liabilities. (Financial Times, Reuters)

Financial Times and Reuters report that Endo shareholders were to receive $80 million in cash and about 49.9% of the combined company, with Mallinckrodt investors owning approximately 50.1%. The new group was described as expecting roughly $3.6 billion in 2025 revenue and planning a listing on the New York Stock Exchange. (FT, Reuters)

Background

Who these companies were before the merger

Both Mallinckrodt and Endo had portfolios that combined branded therapies with significant exposure to generic opioids, and both carried heavy debt loads going into the opioid litigation wave.

Mallinckrodt
Generic opioids, Acthar, and a global settlement framework

Mallinckrodt plc, headquartered in Ireland, was a major manufacturer of generic opioid pain pills and branded products such as Acthar Gel. State attorneys general such as those in California and North Carolina describe Mallinckrodt as one of the largest generic opioid manufacturers in the U.S., and detail a 2020 global settlement framework under which the company agreed to contribute roughly $1.6 billion in structured payments to an opioid abatement trust system. (CA & NC AGs)

Separately, the U.S. Department of Justice and several states brought actions over Medicaid rebates tied to Acthar, alleging that Mallinckrodt and its predecessor Questcor underpaid rebates by treating Acthar as if it had been newly approved in 2013 rather than decades earlier. Those cases resulted in substantial additional payments and civil penalties. (DOJ, state AG releases)

Endo
Opana ER, marketing conduct and restructuring pressure

Endo International plc marketed opioids including Opana and Opana ER. Complaints such as the one filed by the Kentucky Attorney General allege that Endo downplayed addiction risks and overstated benefits, contributing to the opioid epidemic in that state. (Kentucky AG) The FDA later asked Endo to withdraw Opana ER from the market, and Endo complied.

By August 2022, Endo filed for Chapter 11 with roughly $8 billion of debt and extensive opioid-related exposure. (Reuters) A 2024 U.S. Department of Justice press release describes Endo Health Solutions being ordered to pay more than $1.5 billion in criminal fines and forfeiture for distributing misbranded Opana ER, in addition to civil and bankruptcy resolutions funneled through the Endo Public Opioid Trust and related settlement structures. (DOJ, Endo Public Opioid Trust)

Common themes.
Both issuers used Chapter 11 to corral large opioid claim pools into trust structures, reduce debt, and transfer significant equity stakes to lenders and settlement vehicles, while keeping operating pharmaceutical businesses intact.
After the Deal

Keenova & Par Health: two separate shapes

Public materials and industry reporting describe a post-merger structure in which the combined group later separates into a branded therapeutics business and an independent generics/sterile injectables company.

Keenova Therapeutics
Branded specialty medicines

Mallinckrodt’s site and coverage from Pharmaphorum report that after the merger closed on July 31 2025, the company rebranded the specialty therapeutics business as Keenova Therapeutics later in the year. (Mallinckrodt.com, Pharmaphorum) Keenova focuses on branded medicines such as Acthar Gel, Xiaflex and Terlivaz. Pharmaphorum and BioSpace state that Keenova has pro forma 2024 sales of roughly $1.7 billion and around 1,600 employees, with plans for a New York Stock Exchange listing. (Pharmaphorum, BioSpace)

Par Health
Generics, APIs and sterile injectables

The same coverage describes Par Health as the independent generics, API and sterile injectables business spun out of the combined company. BioSpace and related releases indicate that Par Health records quarterly sales of roughly $337 million, employs about 4,000 people, and operates a U.S.-advantaged manufacturing footprint. (BioSpace, Pharmaphorum)

The separation of Keenova and Par Health allows one entity to focus on branded, often higher-margin therapies and the other on volume-driven generics and contract manufacturing, while both sit alongside the pre-existing opioid trust structures.
